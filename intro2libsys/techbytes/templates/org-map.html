The Pattern Research Leadership Model

Organizational Map

{% endblock %} {% block support-content %}

Pat Wagner, of Pattern Research Inc., provides a useful management model for library organizations. Task, managerial, and leadership levels in a library demand similar and different requirements from library systems and and supporting technologies.

Time in Organizational Map

Task Level

Most library operations that deal with specific day-to-day workflows operate at the task level.

Task-level activities focus on accomplishing results in the immediate present. Most library staff tend to operation at the task level, even if the librarian or staff are in middle-management.

The characteristics of a task-based work are:

  • Narrow in scope
  • Not concerned with long-term implications
  • Immediate feedback. (i.e. a patron is either satisfied or not with the reference help, a book is either ordered or not, or backups are accomplished or not)
Some examples include a librarian staffing a reference shift at a service desk, a member of the library staff in acquisition orders material, and the library staff performing back-ups of critical library systems.

Managerial Level

Librarians and staff at the managerial level coordinate the work being done at the task level while communicating with the library leadership or administration about the results of the work being done. This may involve translating the broader ideas and directions from the library administration to the staff's specific task-oriented work-flows. Foresight and planning at the managerial level typically extends no more than 24 months in the future and emphasis is on internal management of the task-level workflows in that time-span.

Some examples of managerial level activities include such things as a Reference Department Head scheduling the next month's reference shifts on a service desk, communicating the summary statistics of a reference interviews in the past year to library administration, and translating the problems reference staff may have with the catalog to the library systems departments.

Leadership Level

The library leadership, usually the Library Director, is more focused on providing a vision for the library that extends significantly beyond the immediate time-horizon of the task level and the two year time horizon of the managerial level. The results strategic decisions made by the Library Director at the leadership level may not be seen in the library until years.

The lack of results are one risk that the leadership must plan for. Other risks including the staff losing trust in the leadership and that viision proves to be a distraction by the task and manager levels. The library director must be able to effectively articulate the vision to the managerial and task levels, to influence not only the library, but also important external stake-holders of the library including town or city councils, university administration, library board members, and donors.

Implications for Library Technology

Decisions about library technology are often made at the managerial and leadership level without proper consideration of all of the impacts the technology may have to the task-level work.

Any library technology must be able to support the workflows and decision-making for each level in the library's organizational map.
